Filed Chapter 7 Before? Here's Your Path.

People who've been through bankruptcy once don't search one question - they search a sequence. We built this page to connect the dots.

1

Check Your Refiling Timeline

Your previous filing creates a waiting period before you can receive another discharge. The clock starts on the date you filed the first case, not when it was discharged.

  • Chapter 7 to Chapter 7: 8 years between filings
  • Chapter 7 to Chapter 13: 4 years between filings
  • Chapter 13 to Chapter 13: 2 years between filings
  • Chapter 13 to Chapter 7: 6 years (with exceptions)

If your previous case was dismissed (not discharged), the 180-day bar under Section 109(g) may apply instead.

2

Choose Your Chapter

Once you know you're eligible, the next question is which chapter. This depends on your income, your debts, and what you're trying to protect.

  • Chapter 7 - Liquidation. Fast (3-4 months), wipes most unsecured debt. You must pass the means test.
  • Chapter 13 - Repayment plan. Takes 3-5 years, but lets you catch up on mortgage/car payments and keep more property.

If you're behind on your mortgage or car loan, Chapter 13 may be your only option to keep them.

3

Understand What You Can Keep

Bankruptcy doesn't mean losing everything. Exemptions protect your property - but the rules vary dramatically by state.

  • Your car: Most states exempt $2,500 to $6,000 in vehicle equity. Chapter 13 lets you "cramdown" the loan if the car is worth less than you owe.
  • Your house: Homestead exemptions range from $5,000 (some states) to unlimited (Texas, Florida). Chapter 13 can cure mortgage arrears over the plan.

4

Run the Means Test

If you're filing Chapter 7, you must pass the means test. This compares your household income to your state's median. If you're above median, you may be forced into Chapter 13 - or you may still qualify with enough deductions.

The means test looks at your last 6 months of income, not your current paycheck. If you recently lost a job or took a pay cut, timing matters.

5

Find the Right Attorney

Not all bankruptcy attorneys are the same. High-volume "bankruptcy mills" handle hundreds of cases with minimal individual attention. Before you hire anyone:

  • Ask how many active cases they're handling right now
  • Ask who will actually appear at your 341 meeting
  • Ask if they'll review your exemptions with you personally
  • Check their track record: use our free screener to see discharge rates by district

A repeat filer's case is more complex than a first-timer's. You need someone who will actually look at the details.

6

Check Your State's Exemptions

Every state has different exemption limits for vehicles, homes, retirement accounts, and personal property. Some states let you choose between state and federal exemptions. This is where cases are won or lost.

The Bottom Line

Filing again is not a failure. Life happens - medical bills, job loss, divorce. The bankruptcy code exists specifically to give people a second chance. But the second time around, you need to get it right.
